About Perkasie

Current Stats

Population:     8,550
Elevation:   436 Feet
Time Zone:    Eastern Standard
Total Area:   2.56 Square Miles

Perkasie, Pennsylvania is a historic borough located in upper Bucks County, halfway between Philadelphia and Allentown. Originally Leni Lenape land, the name comes from the Lenape word Poekskossing, which translates to "one who goes to the place to crack nuts.” Early Dutch settlers pronounced the word with an “r” sound and the name stuck.

Originally established in 1708, it was home of one of William Penn’s county houses. Its economy was largely agrarian, as the rich soil and plentiful rain are a natural fit for farming. However, as the industrial revolution swept through the country, Perkasie became a center of economic activity. A factory at 815 Chestnut Street produced millions of hand-stitched baseballs for the Major League through a contract with Hubbert and later, Spalding.

A popular stop on the train line running between Philadelphia and Allentown, Perkasie was a resort town. Perkasie Park, a collection of Victorian homes and recreation outbuildings, was filled every summer with eager visitors looking to escape the city’s noise and heat. The Perkasie Carousel, Menlo Park, and Lake Nockamixon attractred visitors from far and wide.

During World War II, the half-mile long Perkasie train tunnel, bored from solid rock under Ridge Road, was the busiest train tunnel in the world. Day and night, trains carried freshly-made steel from the Bethlehem Steel to the shipyards in Philadelphia, then returned the empty cars to be refilled. It was the height of Perkasie’s economic prowess.

Later, as the steel industry slowed and tourists branched out to destinations like the Poconos or Florida, Perkasie began to decline. The Spalding factory closed their doors and moved manufacturing to China in the 1980s. Bethlehem steel stopped production and SEPTA discontinued passenger service on the Perkasie spur of track. A devastating fire started by two twelve year old boys in 1988 destroyed 15 percent of the town, including historic buildings from the 19th century. Perkasie’s future looked dire.

Then in 1992, the Perkasie Old Towne Improvement Association was formed to rebuild infrastructure, attract new business, and revitalize the borough. In the subsequent years, a revitalization has swept through the area as new shops, restaurants, and other businesses have opened. The latest venture, a restaurant to be built in the old Perkasie Train Station, is slated to open fall of 2021 and the future looks bright in the borough.
